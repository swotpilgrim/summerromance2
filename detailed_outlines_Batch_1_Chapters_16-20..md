4. **Praise and knowing looks:** Mrs. Henderson praises them both. Betty Ann's teaching methods were creative and effective. Danny's dedication paid off. She notes this proves Danny isn't incapable—he just needed the right approach and someone who believed in him. Mrs. Henderson's eyes are knowing when she looks between them. Their connection has been good for both their academic growth, but she understands there may be complications they're navigating. It's not quite an endorsement of their relationship but it's not disapproval either. It's acknowledgment that she sees them, understands what's developing, and trusts them to handle it maturely. Betty Ann feels both grateful and exposed.

5. **Celebration at the malt shop:** Leaving school, Danny is jubilant—actually, genuinely happy in a way Betty Ann hasn't seen before. He can race! He's eligible for the Fourth of July championship. He won't be expelled. He proved he could do it. On impulse, he suggests they celebrate at the malt shop. Betty Ann hesitates—it's public, on Main Street, people will see them together and talk. But Danny's happiness is infectious and she wants to share this moment. She agrees. They walk into the malt shop together—first time in public as clearly more than just student and tutor. Danny orders two chocolate malts. They sit in a booth, facing each other, grinning. People stare. Betty Ann feels the weight of judgment but also feels defiant. Let them stare. Danny passed. She helped him. This is worth celebrating. Danny reaches across the table and takes her hand openly. She doesn't pull away. In this moment, the risk feels worth it.
